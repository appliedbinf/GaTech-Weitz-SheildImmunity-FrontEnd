Welcome to Shield Immunity

Mitigation and suppression have emerged as the primary means to control and contain spread of Covid-19. Both strategies focus on reducing new infections and both raise questions of sustainability and long-term tactics, given the drastic consequences for socio-economic health and well-being.

The bulk of interventions focus on reducing new transmission by reducing contacts, but what about all those who have already been sick and recovered? Could these prior infections, paradoxically, help reduce collective risk?

In a recent Nature Medicine paper, we proposed and analyzed an epidemiological intervention model that leverages serological tests to identify and deploy recovered individuals who have developed protective antibodies to SARS-CoV-2 as focal points for sustaining safer interactions via interaction substitution.

The objective is to develop sufficient ‘shield immunity’ at the population scale to reduce epidemic intensity. Shield immunity works by amplifying the proportion of interactions with recovered individuals relative to individuals of unknown status. Shield immunity can also be utilized with social distancing measures, potentially enabling the same levels of infection reduction at the population scale with reduced costs of social distancing.

There are significant challenges to developing and implementing interventions that aim to develop population-scale shield immunity. Nonetheless, the magnitude of the current public health and economic crisis demands action, including new approaches to move forward from shelter-in-place orders. Our collaborative team is continuing to explore ways to assess the feasibility of shield immunity that recovered individuals may play a critical, active role in reducing the risk for others, alongside other measures including masks, social distancing, and test/tracing initiatives.
