Frequently Asked Questions

What is shield immunity?

Shield immunity occurs when individuals who are immune are identified as immune are responsible for more interactions than susceptible individuals. Shield immunity tends to reduce interactions between susceptible and infectious people, thereby decreasing the rate of ongoing transmission.

What is the difference between shield immunity and herd immunity?

Herd immunity occurs when a sufficient fraction of the population has acquired immunity to a pathogen, such that transmission would be expected to slow down even if interactions proceeded as normal. In contrast, shield immunity involves changing the frequency of interactions between susceptible and infectious individuals preferentially by immune status. Therefore, shield immunity allows for similar benefits to herd immunity even when only a small fraction of the population has been infected.

How long does immunity last?
At present, the duration of immunity is unknown for COVID-19. Other coronaviruses have immunity of variable duration, with some coronaviruses that cause the common cold having relatively short immunity whereas other coronaviruses, such as the virus that caused the 2009 SARS outbreak, resulting in immunity for up to 3 years.
Can someone who has been infected once with Covid-19 be re-infected?
At present, there are no credible reports of reinfection with Covid-19. Early reports showed that individuals who had apparently recovered from Covid-19 had tested positive for the virus, which raised concerns that it was possible to be re-infected. However, evidence suggests that even if virus can be detected after recovery from illness, such viruses are not able to infect and cause disease in others. Recent studies on the immune response to coronavirus infection have shown that most people who have recovered from Covid-19 have ‘neutralizing’ antibodies that prevent the virus from entering human cells, making it likely that people are protected from repeated infections.
How accurate are serological tests?
The overall accuracy of serological tests depends on the prevalence of infection in the population. Current prevalence in the United States is unknown and most likely varies geographically, but many large-scale surveys are attempting to answer this question. Preliminary data from six locations across the US suggests a true seroprevalence of 1-7% as of April 2020. Based on the best-performing currently approved assays and assuming a prevalence of 5%, scientists estimate that 84-100% of individuals who test positive for antibodies for COVID-19 are truly immune (the positive predictive value, PPV) and 99-100% of individuals who test negative for antibodies for COVID-19 are truly susceptible (negative predictive value, NPV) at the time of testing. If seroprevalence is higher, test results would be more accurate overall.
